Conclusion and Summary

4. Conclusion and Summary

Conclusion

In this project, we used daily data from 2015 to 2025 to study how few financial variables are related to realized volatility of the S&P 500. We constructed a 7-day rolling standard deviation of returns as the outcome, log-transformed it to avoid right-skewness, and fit three models: OLS, Random Forest, and XGBoost.

Now we can answer the research questions proposed at the beginning:

  1. How is implied volatility (VIX) associated with realized volatility?

VIX has the strongest association with realized log-volatility among all predictors. The correlation between VIX and log-volatility is around 0.76, and the OLS coefficient for VIX is positive and highly significant (p < 0.001). Both Random Forest and XGBoost also rank VIX as the most important feature with dominant importance. This makes sense since VIX reflects how uncertain investors expect the market to be in the near future, so higher VIX tends to correlate with higher realized log-volatility.

  1. Is the 10-year Treasury yield (rate) related to changes in realized volatility?

The 10-year Treasury yield has a weaker positive association with realized log-volatility compared to VIX. The OLS coefficient is also statistically significant and the t-statistic is 9.86, smaller than 50.57 for VIX. In term of feature importance, rate ranks second important for XGBoost and Random Forest, sepcifically, 0.0614 importance for Random Forest and 0.0973 importance for XGBoost. So rate is positively related to realized log-volatility, sugguesting that higher interest rates may be associated with increased market volatility.

  1. Do gold prices and the U.S. Dollar Index (DXY) provide additional meaningful information about realized volatility?

Gold is significantly negatively related to realized log-volatility in the OLS model, with a p-value < 0.0001 and coefficient of -0.0001, and the U.S. Dollar Index (DXY) is not significant in the OLS model. Whereas, in the feature importance ranking for both Random Forest and XGBoost, gold is ranked fourth and dxy is ranked third. This sugguests that gold has a significant but very small linear effect on log-volatility, which means as gold price increases, log-volatility decreases slightly. In contrast, dxy is not significant in the OLS model but has a higher importance in Random Forest and XGBoost models, suggesting that dxy may capture some weak, non-linear pattern that are not captured by the linear regression model, but the importance is quite low and may not be meaningful.

In conclusion, gold prices provide some tiny additional negative signal about realized log-volatility, and the U.S. Dollar Index (DXY) does not have a statisitcally siginificant linear relationship with log-volatility or a relatively high importance, so it does not provide substantial exploanation for realized log-volatility.

  1. Do day-of-week effects exist in realized volatility?

No. The boxplot shows very similar distributions of log-volatility across Monday to Friday, with means all around -5 and similar spreads. The pairwise t-tests also confirm this, all pairs of weekdays do not show a significant difference (all p-values > 0.05). So weekday is not a useful predictor in practice.

  1. Which predictors are most important in explaining variation in realized volatility?

VIX is the most important predictor across all three models, it has the highest t-statistic in the OLS model and the highest importance in both Random Forest and XGBoost models. Rate is second, followed by DXY and gold, and the weekday dummies have the lowest importance.

Summary of Findings

Overall, the results suggest that log-volatility is primarily driven by VIX, with some weak positive relationship with US 10-year Treasury Yield and weak negative relationship with gold prices. The U.S. Dollar Index (DXY) and weekday provide very limited information about realized log-volatility.

Comparing the performance of the three models, OLS has the best performance on the test set, sugguesting that the relationship between the predictors and log-volatility is largely linear, more complex models do not provide much improvement.

However, worth noting, the R2 for all three models are relatively low, indicating that a substantial portion of volatility variation remains unexplained by the selected predictors, and we may need to consider more relevant predictors to better explain the realized log-volatility.

Limitations

  • The models are using contemporaneous predictors to model the log-volatility on the same day, rather than strictly predicting the future volatility, so the model should be interpreted as explantory rather than predictive. They can capture the relationships between the predictors and log-volatility from models, but predictive ability is limited.

  • We only used four continuous predictors plus weekday. Many other potentially useful variables are not included, financial market are influenced by many other factors, which are not captured by the selected predictors.

  • The predictors we used are mainly financial metrics of U.S. market, so it may not be generalizable to global market.

  • The analysis is based on log-volatility, so the interpretation of the results can not be directly applied to volatility.

  • The data is time-series data and the models are not time-series models, which may limit their ability to capture the dynamic patterns over time.
